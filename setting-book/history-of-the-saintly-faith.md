# Of the Rise and Governance of the Saintly Faith

*Being an Historical Preface to* On the Side of Heaven, *or, An Account of the Lives of the Imperial Saints*

*Penned by Dr. Archibald Beaumont, Doctor of Sacred Letters of Lord's University*

*Second Edition, Corrected and Enlarged*

---

## A Proem to the Reader

It is no light presumption to gather into a single volume the history of a Faith older than the memory of Men upon this continent. The reader will forgive me, then, where I am obliged to write *it is said* in place of *it is so*; for the criminals of the Repurgo were thorough in their burning, and much that an earlier age took for certain has come down to us only as ash and rumour. What follows I have assembled from the Canon of Saint Avitus, from the epic called *The Tale of the Woodland King*, and from such fragments as the inquisitions did not consign to the flames. Where the sources contend, I have set down the contention honestly, and let the pious reader judge.

## I. Of the First Days, in the Eastern Archipelago

Let it be understood, first and against the common error, that our Faith was not born in Avilund. The Primus Deus Fidelis took its first breath across the eastern waters, upon the Isle of Vander in the Eastern Archipelago, in an age so distant that no honest scholar will assign it a year.

There dwelt a man whose mortal name, the *Tale* preserves, was Riven, though we now know him only by his Imperial name: **Avitus**, the First Saint. His mother, the dogma holds, was Celestial in origin; of his father little is recorded. He had a sister, **Muriel**, and a brother — and it is from this brother, called **Gaul**, that all the early sorrows flow. For Gaul made his pact with the Devils of the Depths, and coveting his brother's place, he slew Avitus's children and worked upon Muriel an outrage so foul that she died of it, a maid of twelve years. The crime was laid wrongly at Avitus's own door, and for it he was cast out of his people.

Yet one child was spared. The seeress **Mairin**, maidservant to Avitus and to his wife **Ruth**, had foreseen the slaughter, and on the night of the murders she exchanged her own infant for the boy **Sever**, and fled with him into the Great Wood. Their long concealment and wandering is the matter of *The Tale of the Woodland King*, which is the foundation-stone of two canons and the dearest poem of our people.

## II. Of the Ascension of Avitus and the First Canon

Avitus, returning at the last to confront his brother, made his peace with Ruth — only for Gaul to bind them both by sorcery and to murder Ruth as he had murdered Muriel. Then did Avitus break his bonds, and the two brothers fought a battle whose length the scholars dispute (some say three days, some thirty-three), until both passed wholly out of this world. Avitus alone of mortal men was bidden by the Angels to dwell, and afterward to rule, in Heaven; and Gaul descended to the Lordship of Hell. It is through the grace of Avitus, won in that ascension, that every later saint has climbed to his station.

From Heaven, Avitus dispatched the angel **Ida** to carry word of these things to his son. Sever, grown, returned out of the Wood to claim the chieftainship of his clan, took the name **Severinius**, and wedded the angel Ida — from which union descends the long line of the Scions, unbroken to the present day. It was Severinius who set down the rudiments of our governance and composed the first **Canon of Saint Avitus**; and so he is rightly called the father of the Church, though never its founder, for that honour belongs to his father alone.

## III. Of the Sancti Primi, the First Saints

Eleven saints we reckon of the first and eldest order, the **Sancti Primi**. These are they who lived and died upon the Archipelago and who never, in life, set foot in Avilund. Chief among them stand Avitus, Severinius, and **Mairin** the seeress; with them are numbered Muriel; Ida the messenger; **Caspar** the old tribal priest, who never in life accepted the Faith yet was sainted for the strength of his convictions; **Caanan**, called the Forgotten; and, restored to their proper place after long and unjust exclusion, **Ruth**, the wife of Avitus, and others whose names — Conrad the defender, Pelagius the shipwright, Viator the voyager — survive now chiefly in the older liturgies.

I must here record, against the prejudice of certain of my predecessors, that the numerologists of an earlier age, in their devotion to the sacred number eleven, contrived to strike both Ruth and the angel Ida from the Pantheon. The present account restores them. Whatever the cabalists may prefer, a Faith that forgets the murdered wife of its First Saint has mistaken arithmetic for reverence.

The candid reader should know that of these eleven, the most are now scarcely worshipped at all. They are too old; their homeland is lost beyond the sea; their cults have dwindled to scholars' footnotes and a relic in a country chapel. Only three of the Primi command a living devotion in our age, and of these I shall speak below.

## IV. Of the Crossing, and the Sancti Secundi

The second order, the **Sancti Secundi**, were born upon the Archipelago but lived out their lives in Avilund; they are the saints of the Crossing. For in an age the historians fix near fourteen centuries before the Imperial reckoning, the peoples of the Islands took ship across the water and came to this continent.

Foremost among them were the two sons of Severinius and Ida: **Dunstan**, the warrior and craftsman, half-angel and fierce; and **Sebald**, the younger, who upon his father's death was acclaimed the second Scion, who chose the site of the great castle Valdenwail, and who is reckoned by many the true builder of the Empire. With them came **Barnabas** the shipwright, and others who carried into a new land the eleven offices their fathers had honoured in the old.

## V. Of the Sancti Coaevi, the First Native-Born

The third order, the **Sancti Coaevi**, were the first saints *born* in Avilund — the children of the Crossing, of eastern blood but not of Avitus's line, contemporaries of the Secundi. Among them is **Ulric**, the very type of the hero, with his war-band about him: his quartermaster **Oswin**, who kept the coin and the provender, and **Cuthman**, once a priest of Saint Tarsis, who buried the last of the Elders at the fall of Waldheim before he took up the soldier's grave-work.

To this order belong also the three colleagues of the **Collegium Waldheim** — **Ignatius**, who was of the elder generation and gathered the others; and the younger **Anselm** and **Gratus** — who, in the last hour of the ancient Elder race, stole into their doomed city and there learned in a few days what would otherwise have perished forever. From their labour descends much of our medicine, our letters, and that lawful art we call the New Magic.

## VI. Of the Four Orders of Sainthood

Thus the saints are reckoned in four orders, after their origin: the **Sancti Primi**, who lived and died in the Archipelago; the **Sancti Secundi**, born there but living here; the **Sancti Coaevi**, the first native-born; and beneath these the countless **Sancti Minores**, the lesser saints, men and women of any origin who wrought some obscure and holy deed in their day and keep, at most, a local following — and, more often, none at all.

## VII. Of the Eleven Offices and the Nine Sects

Within each of the three higher orders, the same eleven offices, or portfolios, are kept: the saints of **Arms** and of **Mercy**; of **Letters** and of **Craft** and of **Trade**; of the **Harvest**, of **Passage**, and of the **Hearth**; of **Death**, of the **Throne**, and of those poor souls we name **the Lost**. So that for every need and station of a mortal life there stands a saint of each generation to intercede.

Yet not all saints are equal in the eyes of the Faithful. In each of the three higher orders, three rise above their fellows; and these nine, the **Sancti Fundatores**, are they about whom the great sects of the Church have grown. Of the Primi, the sects of Avitus, of Severinius, and of Mairin; of the Secundi, the sects of Sebald, of Ignatius, and of Dunstan; of the Coaevi, the sects of Ulric, of Gratus, and of Irenaeus, patron of the Lost. Nine founders, nine sects, three rooted in each generation — and these nine, and not the other four-and-twenty of their orders, are they whom the common Faithful know and name.

## VIII. Of the Faith as it Stands in this Present Age

Let the reader who would understand the Church look to a map of our continent, for the Faith is written upon the land itself.

In the **South** lies the heartland of the old religion: the Imperial Ecclesiarchy of Valdenwail, where the Scion sits in the line of Avitus, and beside it the Bishoprics of St. Severinius and St. Mairin. Here the Sancti Primi are honoured, and the most prominent of the Secundi beside them. In the **Centre** the sects of the Secundi hold sway, with the Bishopric of St. Ignatius and its Collegium. And in the **North**, beneath the Peakes of St. Ulric, the sects of the Coaevi and the rarer saints command the devotion of a rougher and more independent people.

And here, I will confess, lies the deepest fracture in our Faith — one no inquisition has ever mended. For mark which offices the founders keep. The southern sects of the Primi and Secundi are built upon the **Throne** and upon **Letters**: upon rule and upon learning, the offices of authority. But the sects of the Coaevi are built upon none of these. They are the sects of the **Hero**, of the **Healer**, and of the **Lost** — of Arms, of Mercy, and of the forsaken. So that the Church carries, woven into its very founding, the old quarrel between the throne in the South and the people in the North; between the Faith that rules and the Faith that succours. It is a quarrel as old as the Crossing, and I do not think it will be settled in my lifetime, nor in the lifetime of any Scion now living.

*Here ends the Preface.*
