## PREFACE — Of the Rise and Governance of the Saintly Faith

### A Proem to the Reader

It is no light presumption to gather into a single volume the history of a Faith older than the memory of Men upon this continent. The reader will forgive me, then, where I am obliged to write *it is said* in place of *it is so*; for the criminals of the Repurgo were thorough in their burning, and much that an earlier age took for certain has come down to us only as ash and rumour. What follows I have assembled from the Canon of Saint Avitus, from the epic called *The Tale of the Woodland King*, and from such fragments as the inquisitions did not consign to the flames. Where the sources contend, I have set down the contention honestly, and let the pious reader judge.

### I. Of the First Days, in the Eastern Archipelago

Let it be understood, first and against the common error, that our Faith was not born in Avilund. The Primus Deus Fidelis took its first breath across the eastern waters, upon the Isle of Vander in the Eastern Archipelago, in an age so distant that no honest scholar will assign it a year.

There dwelt a man whose mortal name, the *Tale* preserves, was Riven, though we now know him only by his Imperial name: **Avitus**, the First Saint. His mother, the dogma holds, was Celestial in origin; of his father little is recorded. He had a sister, **Muriel**, and a brother — and it is from this brother, called **Gaul**, that all the early sorrows flow. For Gaul made his pact with the Devils of the Depths, and coveting his brother's place, he slew Avitus's children and worked upon Muriel an outrage so foul that she died of it, a maid of twelve years. The crime was laid wrongly at Avitus's own door, and for it he was cast out of his people.

Yet one child was spared. The seeress **Mairin**, maidservant to Avitus and to his wife **Ruth**, had foreseen the slaughter, and on the night of the murders she exchanged her own infant for the boy **Sever**, and fled with him into the Great Wood. Their long concealment and wandering is the matter of *The Tale of the Woodland King*, which is the foundation-stone of two canons and the dearest poem of our people.

### II. Of the Ascension of Avitus and the First Canon

Avitus, returning at the last to confront his brother, made his peace with Ruth — only for Gaul to bind them both by sorcery and to murder Ruth as he had murdered Muriel. Then did Avitus break his bonds, and the two brothers fought a battle whose length the scholars dispute (some say three days, some thirty-three), until both passed wholly out of this world. Avitus alone of mortal men was bidden by the Angels to dwell, and afterward to rule, in Heaven; and Gaul descended to the Lordship of Hell. It is through the grace of Avitus, won in that ascension, that every later saint has climbed to his station.

From Heaven, Avitus dispatched the angel **Ida** to carry word of these things to his son. Sever, grown, returned out of the Wood to claim the chieftainship of his clan, took the name **Severinius**, and wedded the angel Ida — from which union descends the long line of the Scions, unbroken to the present day. It was Severinius who set down the rudiments of our governance and composed the first **Canon of Saint Avitus**; and so he is rightly called the father of the Church, though never its founder, for that honour belongs to his father alone.

### III. Of the Four Orders of Sainthood

The saints are reckoned in four orders, after their origin.

The **Sancti Primi**, the First Saints, lived and died upon the Archipelago and never in life set foot in Avilund. Chief among them stand Avitus, Severinius, and **Mairin** the seeress; with them are numbered **Muriel**, the murdered sister of the First Saint; **Ida** the messenger; **Caspar** the old tribal priest; **Tarsis**, keeper of the graves; and, restored to their proper place after long and unjust exclusion, **Ruth**, the wife of Avitus. The remaining offices are held by saints — Conrad the defender, Pelagius the shipwright, Viator the voyager — whose names survive now chiefly in the older liturgies.

I must here record, against the prejudice of certain of my predecessors, that the numerologists of an earlier age, in their devotion to the sacred number eleven, contrived to strike both Ruth and the angel Ida from the Pantheon. The present account restores them. Whatever the cabalists may prefer, a Faith that forgets the murdered wife of its First Saint has mistaken arithmetic for reverence.

The **Sancti Secundi**, the Second Saints, were born upon the Archipelago but lived out their lives in Avilund; they are the saints of the Crossing, when, near fourteen centuries before the Imperial reckoning, the peoples of the Islands took ship across the water to this continent. The **Sancti Coaevi**, the Coevals, were the first saints *born* in Avilund — of eastern blood but not of Avitus's line, contemporaries of the Secundi. And beneath all these stand the countless **Sancti Minores**, the lesser saints, men and women of any origin who wrought some obscure and holy deed in their day, and who keep, at most, a local following — and, more often, none at all.

### IV. Of the Eleven Offices and the Nine Sects

Within each of the three higher orders the same eleven offices are kept: the saints of **Arms** and of **Mercy**; of **Letters**, of **Craft**, and of **Trade**; of the **Harvest**, of **Passage**, and of the **Hearth**; of **Death**, of the **Throne**, and of those poor souls we name **the Lost**. So that for every need and station of a mortal life there stands a saint of each generation to intercede.

Yet not all saints are equal. In each higher order, three rise above their fellows; and these nine, the **Sancti Fundatores**, are they about whom the great sects of the Church have grown — nine founders, nine sects, three rooted in each generation. Of the Primi: the sects of Avitus, Severinius, and Mairin. Of the Secundi: of Sebald, Ignatius, and Dunstan. Of the Coaevi: of Ulric, Gratus, and Irenaeus.

Mark well which offices these founders keep, for in it lies the deepest fracture of our Faith. The southern sects of the Primi and Secundi are built upon the **Throne** and upon **Letters** — upon rule and upon learning, the offices of authority — and they cluster in the South, about the Imperial Ecclesiarchy of Valdenwail and the Bishoprics of St. Severinius and St. Mairin, with the Secundi sects holding the Centre. But the Coaevi sects, who hold the rough and independent North beneath the Peakes of St. Ulric, are built upon none of these. They are the sects of the **Hero**, the **Healer**, and the **Lost** — of Arms, of Mercy, and of the forsaken. So the Church carries, woven into its very founding, the old quarrel between the throne in the South and the people in the North; between the Faith that rules and the Faith that succours. I do not think it will be settled in my lifetime, nor in the lifetime of any Scion now living.

## BOOK ONE — The Sancti Primi

*The First Saints, who lived and died upon the Archipelago. By the present age, the most are all but forgotten; only the three Fundatores — Avitus, Severinius, and Mairin — keep a living devotion.*

**Avitus** *(Throne)* — The First Saint, whose mortal name was Riven. Cast out for a crime he did not commit, he transcended this world in his final battle with his brother Gaul, and alone of mortal men was bidden to rule in Heaven. Patron of fatherhood and of authority itself; the wellspring from whom every later saint draws grace. The Empire bears his name, and the Scion rules in his line from the Imperial Ecclesiarchy of Valdenwail.

**Severinius** *(Letters)* — Sever, son of Avitus and Ruth, hidden in the Great Wood by Mairin and raised to manhood there. The first Scion, who returned to rule his clan, set down the Church's governance, and composed the Canon of Saint Avitus. Patron of tradition, of letters, and of the administrators and middling ranks who keep the world in order. His is among the largest followings in the Empire; his Bishopric lies in the South.

**Mairin** *(Harvest)* — The seeress and maidservant who foresaw the slaughter, exchanged her own child for Sever, and bore him into the Wood. A mother-figure to the common people; patron of agriculture, of families and children, and of prognostication. Her canon is vast and well-ordered, a web of monasteries and convents; her Bishopric anchors the southern heartland.

**Caspar** *(Mercy)* — The old tribal priest of the clan's pagan rites, who opposed the new Faith to his death and was sainted nonetheless for the iron of his convictions. The southern canons remember him as keeper of the healing-craft and the lore of herbs, for the priest was ever also the healer. A shadowed, hermit-haunted cult, and a controversial one.

**Pelagius** *(Craft)* — "Of the sea." The master shipwright of the Archipelago, whose vessels first dared the open water, and whose craft the Secundi saint Barnabas would inherit. Little worshipped now, but honoured wherever a keel is laid.

**Viator** *(Trade)* — "The voyager." The saint of the first sea-trade, whose ships carried goods between the scattered islands and bound the Archipelago into one people before the Crossing.

**Conrad** *(Arms)* — The foundational warrior-defender of the clan in its earliest and most perilous days — the shield raised against the dark of the Archipelago. His name survives chiefly in the old battle-liturgies.

**Ida** *(Passage)* — The angel dispatched by Avitus from Heaven to bear his word to Severinius, and condemned to die on Earth for the errand; she wedded Severinius and mothered Dunstan and Sebald. Patron of messengers and of all who travel. Her worship has dwindled, and the schoolmen still dispute whether she was truly a saint at all — yet this account numbers her among the Primi without apology.

**Ruth** *(Hearth)* — Wife of Avitus, murdered by Gaul as Muriel was murdered. Struck from the Pantheon by the numerologists and named *interdicto fides*, yet her worship would never die among the women of the Empire. Restored here to her rightful office: patron of the household, the marriage-bond, and the grieving wife.

**Tarsis** *(Death)* — The undertaker of the Archipelago, keeper of the rites of burial and the peace of the dead. His priests speak the words over the fallen and tend the graves; it was a priest of Tarsis, the Coaevus Cuthman, who would one day lay the last of the Elders to rest.

**Muriel** *(The Lost)* — Sister of Avitus, outraged and murdered by their brother Gaul, dead at twelve years. The archetype of the innocent wronged; patron of the violated, the murdered, and the children who die before their time. Her following is small and almost wholly female, kept in convents — and, it is whispered, in a hidden order of nuns who hunt the murderer and the ravisher to their deaths.

## BOOK TWO — The Sancti Secundi

*The Second Saints, born upon the Archipelago but living their lives in Avilund — the saints of the Crossing and the settlement. Their sects hold the Centre of the continent.*

**Sebald** *(Throne)* — Younger son of Severinius and Ida, half-angel and long-lived; the second Scion. He founded the great castle of Valdenwail and, conquering and assimilating the neighbouring clans, is reckoned the true builder of the Empire. Patron of nobility, of primogeniture, and of the right of inheritance. The Peakes of St. Sebald stand north of Waldheim.

**Ignatius** *(Letters)* — The elder of the three Colleagues of Waldheim, who gathered his younger fellows and stole into the Elders' doomed city to wrest their knowledge from them in the last hour before its fall. Patron of knowledge, academia, explorers, and cartographers. His Bishopric lies in the Centre, and his Society still excavates the ruins of Waldheim.

**Dunstan** *(Arms)* — Eldest son of Severinius and Ida, half-angel, fierce in war and gifted in the forge. Patron of soldiers, craftsmen, athletes, and the artillery; his is a crusading and innovating sect, credited (though some doubt it) with the invention of gunpowder. The Cathedral of St. Dunstan stands at Lysander, and the Republic of Dunstanmoore bears his name.

**Alodia** *(Mercy)* — Patron of healers and herbalists. She carried the herb-craft of old Caspar out of the priesthood and into the hands of the village healers, that the settlers of a new land might be tended.

**Barnabas** *(Craft)* — A native of the Archipelago, friend of Dunstan and Sebald, and captain of the legendary vessel *Bram'Cairn*. Patron of sailors and shipwrights. He, like Ruth, was once cast out of the Pantheon by the arithmetic of the numerologists, and is here restored.

**Aquila** *(Trade)* — "The eagle." Patron of merchants — the sharp-eyed trader who built the markets of the settled continent and bound its towns in commerce.

**Eligius** *(Harvest)* — Patron of farmers, ranchers, and the labour of the field. His is the practical husbandry that broke the soil of Avilund and fed the growing colonies.

**Gabrielle** *(Passage)* — Patron of messengers and couriers; the mortal echo of the angel Ida's heavenly errand, whose runners and heralds held the scattered settlements of the Crossing together.

**Adelle** *(Hearth)* — Patron of lovers. Where Ruth keeps the marriage-bond and the grieving wife, Adelle keeps the warmth of courtship and the joy of the settled hearth.

**Killian** *(Death)* — Patron of the hunters of the undead. In a haunted land where the dead would not lie quiet, his was no gravedigger's office but a soldier's: to put down what rose from the barrows of the Elder dead.

**Stanis** *(The Lost)* — Patron of martyrs — of those who suffered and died for the Faith in the age of its spreading, when the Word was carried among the Ferals and the unconverted at the cost of blood.

## BOOK THREE — The Sancti Coaevi

*The Coevals, the first saints born in Avilund — of eastern blood but not of Avitus's line, contemporaries of the Secundi. Their sects hold the rough North.*

**Ulric** *(Arms)* — The very type of the hero: wholly mortal, friend and rival of the half-angel Dunstan, raised to sainthood by a long train of legendary deeds. Patron of personal glory, of ambition, and of the questing knight — and, in our own commercial age, of the merchant-houses, whose young men now seek their glory on the battlefields of trade and finance. The Peakes of St. Ulric crown the North.

**Gratus** *(Mercy)* — One of the three Colleagues of Waldheim; patron of medicine and the physician's art, which he is said to have learned in part from the dying Elders. His is a great and respected canon throughout the Empire.

**Irenaeus** *(The Lost)* — Patron of the lost, the strayed, and the forsaken. His shrine, the place called St. Irenaeus, would in a later age be the site of a famous and terrible massacre — a fate that only deepened the devotion of those who name him in their darkest hours.

**Anselm** *(Letters)* — The younger of Ignatius's two Colleagues; patron of the New Magic, the lawful art wrested from the Elders before their fall. From his canon descends much of what the Empire knows of the disciplined working of power.

**Bernward** *(Craft)* — Patron of architects and engineers. Where Pelagius and Barnabas raised ships, Bernward raised walls and bridges and the first great works of stone in the new land.

**Oswin** *(Trade)* — Ulric's quartermaster, who kept the coin, the provender, and the supply of the hero's war-band. From his humble office descends, in time, the whole commercial strand of Ulric's cult; the ledger was always carried at the warrior's side.

**Judith** *(Harvest)* — Wife of Sebald, and the agricultural saint of the conquering temper. Where Mairin honours cultivation as the furthering of life, Judith honours it as the triumph of Man over Nature. Patron of farmers, of women in their own right, and of the breeders of beasts; her following is strong and rural, and her clerics keep the bloodlines of the Empire's finest stock.

**Colman** *(Passage)* — Patron of rangers and trackers; the saint of the wilderness ways and the northern marches, where the road gives out and only the woodsman's craft will serve.

**Agatha** *(Hearth)* — Patron of women in childbirth and of the bearing of children. Hers is the office of the new generations, the native-born sons and daughters of a land that was no longer foreign to them.

**Cuthman** *(Death)* — Once a priest of Saint Tarsis who served among the Colleagues at Waldheim, and who with his own hands buried the last of the Elders when their city fell. Afterward he laid down the scholar's robe and followed Ulric to war, tending the dead of the war-band. "The famous man," his name signifies — and yet, by the cruel irony of the ages, he is now among the most forgotten of his order.

**Leopold** *(Throne)* — Patron of nobles, aristocrats, and royalty; the saint of the settled aristocracy that rose once the conquering was done and the great houses took their lands and titles.

## BOOK FOUR — Of the Sancti Minores

Beyond the three-and-thirty of the higher orders stretch the **Sancti Minores**, the lesser saints — countless, and of every origin. Some did one holy deed in an obscure corner of the world and were never heard of again; some keep a single chapel in a single valley; the most have no living worshipper at all, and survive only as a name in a parish register or a relic in a forgotten reliquary.

Among them, and not the least, is **Caanan**, whom the older accounts (this author's own first edition among them) once numbered with the early saints. Yet the truth, which honesty compels, is that Caanan has no canon, no order, and no known follower; his name is invoked only by the common man when misfortune strikes, and the schoolmen are divided whether he be a saint at all or a Hellion in saint's clothing. He is rightly the patron of the very thing he has become: the Forgotten. Let him stand, then, first among the Minores — a caution to every saint that even a place among the early Pantheon is no proof against the long erosion of the years.

*Here ends the Account.*
