## The World in Brief

Avilund is a continent with a long memory and a short attention span.

It has been home to at least three civilizations before this one. The most recent were the Elders — an enigmatic, long-lived people of extraordinary sophistication who were wiped out two thousand years ago by the Ferals, a broad category of humanoid creatures that still lurk at the edges of civilization. Men arrived late, inherited a continent full of abandoned Elder cities, half-understood Elder knowledge, and a war that wasn't theirs.

They built an Empire out of it anyway.

That Empire is now fractured. A revolution four hundred years ago broke its political grip on the northern and central territories. The church that held it together still exists, still has teeth, but no longer has a crown. What came after the revolution is what this setting is about: an age of learning, of exploration, of competing ideas — and of all the old things that didn't go away just because the calendar changed.

## Tone and Feel

This is **low fantasy**. There are no dragons. No gods stride the earth. Magic exists — and the supernatural is genuinely present in the world — but it is rare, distrusted, and never comfortable. A miracle is not a spectacle. A demon is not a set piece. Both are deeply unsettling encounters with something that should not be.

The setting has three distinct registers running at once, mapped roughly to historical feel:

- **Avitus** (the central hub city) feels like **London, circa 1750** — printing presses, universities, banks, corporations, early industry, cosmopolitan politics, and a thin overlay of occult activity that respectable people pretend isn't there.
- **The Southern polities** feel like **1350s Europe** — feudal, ecclesiastical, steeped in tradition, and suspicious of what's happening up north.
- **The North** feels like **1050 Scandinavia** — tribal, harsh, pre-feudal, and operating by entirely different rules.

All three exist simultaneously, and characters move between them.

## The Faith

The Saintly Faith is the dominant religion across most of Avilund. It holds that the souls of the dead must travel through the Valley of Shadow — beset by demons — to reach Heaven, and that the Saints act as intercessors for the journey. Venerate the right saint in life, and they will help you in death.

There are hundreds of saints. Different regions worship different ones. The church is not one thing — it is dozens of regional canons, competing interests, political factions, and local traditions, all nominally united under a supreme head called the Scion, whose power has dramatically diminished since the revolution.

In the north, the Kells hold an entirely different faith — animistic, ancestral, with no overlap with the southern pantheon at all.

**None of these faiths are confirmed true.** Clerics can perform miracles. The supernatural is real. But no theology has a verified monopoly on what actually happens after death, where the universe came from, or what the Saints really are. These are things for players to encounter, argue about, and live inside — not questions with predetermined answers.

## Magic

Magic in Avilund has a history as long as the Elders and a reputation about as welcome.

The Elders practiced it on a scale Men have never matched, and in two distinct forms: an old, communal magic tied to nature, and a refined, academic magic concerned with mastery of those same forces. Men inherited fragments of the second kind when they occupied Elder cities, and for several centuries it was studied openly.

Then came the inquisitions.

The church launched two of them over the course of several hundred years, hunting down practitioners of what it called the Black Faith — a term broad enough to cover genuine demon-worshippers and curious scholars alike. The second inquisition, called the Repurgo, lasted 138 years and destroyed an incalculable amount of knowledge. Books were burned atop pyres made of more books.

The Repurgo ended with the revolution. The Age of Enlightenment followed. Magic is, strictly speaking, legal now. But the public distrust never fully healed. Those known to practice it are given a wide berth. Those who practice it well tend not to advertise it.

## Player Characters

All player characters are human. This is not a setting with elves, dwarves, or halflings as playable options.

The Elders are extinct — an elf-analogue, but extinct, and never to be confused with the fantasy archetype. Dwarves exist but are extremely rare and foreign. The Ferals are bipedal humanoids of enormous variety who are overwhelmingly antagonists, never player characters.

The Kells are Men — a distinct cultural group from the eastern archipelago, rough and warlike, now occupying the far north. Not a separate race, just a different people.

## Discovery

The world is encountered through the documents that people in it actually wrote.

The setting's primary documents are in-world texts: a university history primer, an encyclopaedia of magical artifacts, a traveler's guide, church records, a revolutionary broadsheet, a saint's journal, a heresy trial transcript. These are the things players read to understand where they are. They are biased, partial, culturally situated, and sometimes wrong.

That is intentional. There is no manual for Avilund. There is just evidence, and the conclusions you draw from it.
