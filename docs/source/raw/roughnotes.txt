I think that making this distinction gives us two advantages.
1. Medicine and science suddenly have a role to play in our world. Diseases can be cured by Clerics and Paladins, but there are only a handful of those around, and most, since they are special members of the church, are too busy doing their superior's bidding to be bothered with curing plagues and setting bones. cleric back in town. But is it easier to fight when there is no way to heal other than plain old bed rest, or a surgeon's deft hands? Gregor would do well in our world.
2. It releases the PC cleric characters from having to be typecast as the parish priest. This was the original intention of AD&D 2ed, where Clerics were crusaders and special forces, so to speak, of the larger church. Most priests were just regular joes, but Clerics were special. 1. The Most Holy and Supreme Speaker of Our Faith ("The Speaker" for short)
This is the patriarch and supreme leader of the faith.
2. The Most Holy Prelates of the Faith ("Prelate" for short)
These are the commanders or leaders of each Saintly Canon. Thus, The Most Holy Prelate of Saint Maturin of Anauroch (or "Prelate Boregar of Saint Maturin" for short) is the highest ranking member of Saint Maturin's sect. He can be a Cleric, Paladin, Monk, Priest, Aristocrat or whatever else, and of any level.
3. Priors, Abbots, & Deans

These are the commanders of individual monasteries (Abbots) knightly orders (Priors) or cathedrals and societies (Deans). They usually have some Brothers and Initiates under their command, as well as lay individuals that do not fall within the heirarchy of the church. they report directly to their respective Prelate within their Canon.
A "Prior" leads a Knightly order and can be referred to as "Prior", "Master", or "Father" depending on who is addressing him. Formally, he might be "Boregar, Prior of the Crusading Order of Saint Maturin", and would be referred to as such by thoise outside the church. To the knights under his command, he would be known simply as "Master Boregar". To those within the church, but who are not part of the knightly order, he would be known simply as "Father Boregar".
An "Abbot" heads a particular monastery, and would be referred to in the same fashion as with a Prior. (ie: "Abbot Boregar of the Monastery of Saint Maturin the Martyred"; "Master Boregar", "Father Boregar".)
A "Dean" heads a cathedral and its province, or a Society of Clerics, and would be referred to in the same fashion as both a Prior and an Abbott. (ie: "Boregar, Dean of the Cathedral of Saint Maturin in the Province of Eastwitch" or "Boregar, Dean of the Society of Saint Maturin of Anauroch"; "Master Boregar"; "Father Boregar".)
4. Priests, Monks & Knights ("Brothers" for short)
These are the rank and file members of the church. They fill most roles, and provide the starting rank for most PC's and NPC's. Whether a Cleric, Priest, Monk, Paladin, Knight or whathave-you, they are referred to as "Brother", or, more formally, as "Brother of Saint Maturin" reflecting the individual Saint they serve, if they serve a Saint.
5. Initiates
These are students, squires, etc who are on their way to becoming higher ranking members of the church.
6. Warden
Wardens are lay-individuals who are not formally part of the church hierarchy, but who are either employed, or serve within, the church itself. Most wardens are lay-preachers who assist a priest or an initiate in large congregations, or they may be administrative staff within the church offices.

This is what I figure we need at this point, before we go much further:
1. A Calendar
It may seem a minor thing, but if we put a calendar in place now, we can accurately date everything that we are detailing. It's nice, if for nothing else than as an outline of topics, to have a chronology.
2. A List of Place Names
This should be more or less uniform. I think we should put a reasonably logical nomenclature in place for most of our geography. I like the idea of having, essentially, four languages in the history of our world. (The names of them are just placeholders until we can come up with better ones):
a. Elderspeech: this was the language of the Elders, obviously, and the oldest around. Very rare and obscure, found mainly in older magical writings. An old magic sword, for example, would have writing in Elderspeech inscribed upon the blade. No one really speaks it at all, for it is near impossible to get the pronunciation right. Those who can speak it, usually do so in a bastardised way. I was planning on using the Arabic script for this language, or even Chinese-like characters (that is, it is a completely different alphabet and grammar from the rest of the languages, making it very difficult for a character to learn it. A character can learn it, but it should cost double the number of language slots to learn to read it, and another double to speak it properly. That is, then, a total of 2 language slots to read it, and a total of 4 language slots to read and speak it. In order to have this kind of command of the language, then, it requires an INT of at least 18.)
b. Tribalspeech: this was the early language of humans, akin to Celtic, Scandinavian or maybe German. Our first Saints and most Barbarians and even the Orcs (which I'm leaning to call them Ferals, but more on that in a bit) speak a variant dialect of it now. Some written forms of the original version of it can still be found on magic items and religious relics. It forms the basis of the newer languages in a rough sort of way. There are as many dialects and variants as there are tribes.
c. Old Imperial: This is essentially Latin. The vast majority of writings are in this language. For 2000 years, or so, this was the language in which the entire empire operated. It never really died, though it was highly modified as colonists and explorers and others began adapting words and phrases from the barbaric Tribalspeech tongues. This intermixing between Old Imperial and Tribalspeech dialects led to the informal formation of what is considered "New Imperial".
d. New Imperial: This is essentially English. New Imperial is the language currently in use by our civilisation. It has grown out of mixing Tribalspeech dialects with Old Imperial as a result of colonisations and barbarian inter-marriages and influences. It is

usually just refered to as "Imperial", and it is the language of the common man. Old Imperial is still the language of goverment, the church, academics and the high born.
3. Names of Demographic Groups
You are right, we need to start getting some of these down. This is what I suggest:
a. Saints: they are just called "Saints".
b. Celestials: people call them Celestials or Angels. Although, the term "Celestial" refers to any being who dwells in Celestia (so a Saint is also a "Celestial"), whereas "Angel" refers specifically to those beings who are native to Celestia. People don't much care for the difference, but scholars do.
c. Hellion: these are what we were previously calling "Anti-Saints"
d. Devils: people call them Fiends or Devils. Same as with Celestials, "Fiends" refers to any being who dwells in Hell (so our Hellions are also Fiends), whereas "Devils" are the natives of Hell.
e. Demons: these can be anything and everything the common man wants to label as such. Strictly speaking, natives of the Abyss are referred to as "Abyssals" or "Tanar'ri". The same distinctions apply as with Celestials and Fiends.
f. Celestialspawn: these are strong beings who have one mortal parent and one Angelic parent (ie Half-Angels or Half-Celestials.) They are creatures of legend, though, they were real enough, as there is evidence of them from their descendants.
